NASA (National Aeronautic and Space Administration) is a government program in the United States, that strives to better understand our planet and our universe. In order to gain such knowledge, NASA has been launching satellites into earth's orbit and deep space since 1958 with Explorer 1. To read more about Explorer 1's journey click here: https://www.jpl.nasa.gov/jplhistory/early/firstsatellites.php. Since 1958 NASA has launched over 500 satllites that coninuously obtain data until they are no longer able to, some having a lifespan of over 20 years! Some satellites are thrusted into deep space to explore our solar system. Missions like Juno and Cassini-Huygens are satellites that have successfully collected data on our gas giants in our solar system. The Cassini-Huygens satellite's focus of study is on the ringed planet Saturn and its moons. This satellite was satellite launched October 15, 1997. Almost a full 20 years have passed since it began exploring space, and it is now on its final leg. The Cassini-Huygens satellite is scheduled to end its tenure orbiting Saturn on April 26, 2017 by taking a final plunge into Saturn's atmosphere. The goal is that Cassini-Huygens will have the ability to analyze the atmospheric composition and create potential image analysis for further processing, and the first ever view of Saturn beneath the gas. To look further into Cassini-Huygen's journey click here: https://www.nasa.gov/mission_pages/cassini/main/index.html

Likewise, the Juno mission set it's sights on our solar system's biggest planet, Jupiter. The Juno satellite was launched in 2011, and made it's arrival to Jupiter in July 2016. Since then, Juno has just completed its fifth orbit around Jupiter. Juno's goal for this mission is to gather climatlogical data and atmospheric composition data. This will help understand the physics of this red gas giant, and further our knowledge within our solar system. To learn more about the discoveries and goals of Juno's mission click here: https://www.nasa.gov/mission_pages/juno/main/index.html or watch the video provided below!

A bit closer to home, NASA has many Earth orbiting satellites that are constantly collecting data utilizing imagery and sensors. Observing Earth from a bird's eye view can be quite beneficial in learning how humans, animals, plants or natural cycles are affecting each other in our enormous Earth system. What's special about these satellites is that they are equipped with various sensors or cameras that can detect many different attributes on Earth. For instance NASA's LandSat Satellites were the first series of satellitesin charge og collecting imagery of the Earth. Since LandSat-5 launched in 1982, the cameras attached can now detect all relfected waves of the electromagnetic spectrum. While images using bands that fall under the visile light interval are applied in applications like GoogleEarth, other bands of surface relfectance can help analysis in many scientific fields. The most common use of imagery not using a red, blue, green visible light scheme is a near infrared scheme, where vegetation/crops can easily be detected as well as the status of their health.

The Geostationary Operational Environmental Satellite-N/O/P (GOES N-P), helps detect large scale environmental changes. This helps scientist view environments and their changes overtime, helping create environmental forecasting analysis on the observed changes. Creating environmental forecasting models can help predict certain changes when certain stimuli enters a system. Mostly severe weather is what is monitored in the GOES N-P mission.
The LandSat series satellites, previously mentioned on the website, has a main purpose of collecting imagery of the Earth using all wavelengths in the Electromagnetic Spectrum. What this means is that every object on Earth reflects certain colors at different wavelengths. Experimenting with different bands caputured by the satellite can create various analysis on different objects.
The Terra Satellite is equipped with many instruments that detect a numerous amount of meteorological data that is a key for NASA Earth Science discoveries. I personally have used the MODIS intsrument located on the Terra Satellite. This instrument detects low resolution precipitation levels that can help analyze drought, flooding, vegetation densities, and can help analyze other scientific phenomena.
